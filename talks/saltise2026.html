Abstract

Clarity is key when communicating to students the “do’s and don’ts” of Generative AI use during assessments. Establishing a set of coherent descriptions, accompanied by easy-to-recognize visuals, can reduce stress and confusion among students. However, making and communicating such choices can be stressful and confusing for teachers with diverse skillsets and attitudes towards AI.

In this talk, we share experiences of the adoption of a five-level AI assessment scale at college level — from no AI use permitted through to AI as a necessary ingredient of the work itself — including the steps, the obstacles, and the concrete lessons learned.

Description

Following in the footsteps of many other academic institutions, our college, along with its international sibling entities, is in the process of adopting a five-level scale for indicating within the instructions of each graded assessment what type of GenAI use is permitted. The levels range from forbidding all AI use to free use of all AI tools, topping things off with a final level that incorporates AI use into the assessment preparation as a necessary ingredient.

The difficulties start with defining the levels in a way that is multidisciplinary and yet clear and concise enough to help inform the students. Encouraging teachers to associate a level to each graded assessment is the next big step, accommodating both those eager to adopt or forbid as well as those unsure about the role AI should play. Once choices are made, they must be discussed among teachers of parallel or sequential groups to achieve a consistent student experience — posing challenges for academic liberty. Students then need to be informed of the implications for how they carry out their tasks.

Drawing on an early 2026 rollout, this talk shares experiences on how assessments map to each level, where and how redesign takes place, what the frequent pain points are, and how disciplines appear to differ in their pace and needs.

Three non-negotiables in all AI-assisted work
  • attribution: cite the tool and prompt used
  • validation: verify and correct AI outputs
  • assessing process over product: value decisions and approach, not just the final result
